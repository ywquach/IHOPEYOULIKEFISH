Hierophany

“Hierophany” refers to the concept of the sacred interacting with the profane in ways which make it tangible, and revealing itself to us.

Myths and legends often center instances of hierophany. Often describing manifestations of the sacred in the ordinary or “profane” through mythical places, events, objects, or creatures.

In modern times, distinction of the sacred and profane persists but transforms, taking on a manufactured inaccessibility which allows for the commercialization of the sacred. This manufactured exclusivity then simultaneously alienates the sacred from the people whilst eroding the untouchability of the divine by subjecting it to the same commercial logics as any other institution.

Alternatively, within the context of historical mythos, the sacred and the profane are found to often operate in an inverse relationship: the sacred is not distant or inaccessible, but actively embedded within the profane, providing meaning to events and forces that would otherwise appear inexplicable.

Below are some instances of a more historical understanding of hierophany, wherein the sacred manifests as a tangible and accessible: fish.

The Sacred

“The Sacred” (Sacrum) are things, places, or times set apart, revered, and imbued with spiritual meaning, or a connection to the divine.

They inspire reverence, and are often seen as transcendent — containing deeper meaning beyond their immediate material component.

The Profane

"The Profane" (Pro-fanum) refer to the ordinary, everyday aspects of life — that lack sacred meaning, contrasting the "sacred" which is associated with the spiritual and often revered.

The profane signifies things outside the divine realm, from common talk to outright disrespect.

ONAMAZU

Ōnamazu are giant catfish said to dwell in muddy waters, where they thrash about when agitated or excited—much like ordinary catfish.

Ōnamazu are associated with earthquakes, an association which emerged from frequent sightings of catfish convulsing violently without apparent cause shortly before tremors would strike. By the Edo period, this led to the image of a giant restless catfish gradually displacing the idea of underground dragon in popular imagination, becoming the prevailing explanation for seismic unrest and natural disaster.

SHACHIHOKO

Shachihoko are fearsome sea creatures with the body of a large fish and the head of a tiger, they known for their upward-curving tails, spiked dorsal fins, and ability to gulp vast amounts of water or summon rain clouds.

Shachihoko are thought to be protector spirits originating from the colder northern seas. They originated from china and became powerful protective symbols in Japan during the Nara and Heian period.

Commonly installed in pairs on the rooftops of castles, temples, gates, and samurai residences, it was thought that in the event of a fire the shachihoko would be able to call down rain or expel the stored water in their stomach.

NINGYO

Ningyo are monstrous humanoid fish whose forms range from child-sized creatures with distorted, fishlike faces to larger beings with human torsos, long bony fingers, and sharp claws—far closer to beasts than to the beautiful Western-style mermaids that became popular in Japan after the Edo period.

Mentioned as early as the Nihon Shoki (619 CE), ningyo appear throughout Japan’s oldest histories, often as omens or sources of danger. Their flesh is said to grant eternal youth, making them the subject of many tales, yet attempting to capture or harm a ningyo risks a devastating curse; some stories recount entire towns swallowed by earthquakes or tidal waves after a fisherman brought one home.
